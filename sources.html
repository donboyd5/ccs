NYSED data files

The enrollment and staffing numbers are built from two New York State Education Department databases, both downloaded from https://data.nysed.gov/downloads.php and distributed as annual Microsoft Access files:

  • Enrollment database (New York State Education Department, 2025a) — the BEDS Day Enrollment table, which gives K-12 enrollment by district. Coverage on this website: school years 2015–16 through 2024–25. Enrollment is the BEDS-day count (first Wednesday of October).
  • Student & Educator (“STUDED”) database (New York State Education Department, 2025b) — the Staff table, which gives teacher, principal, counselor, and social-worker counts plus teacher attendance and turnover. Coverage: school years 2017–18 through 2024–25. Because teacher counts start in 2017–18, any measure that uses teachers (such as students per teacher) begins at school-year-ending
    1. The same database’s Average Class Size table — roster-based class sizes (students ÷ sections) for K-2 and the State-tested grade/subject and Regents courses, school years 2018–19 through 2024–25 — backs the class-size charts; it is measured from course rosters, not teacher counts, and NYSED’s pre-2018-19 (teacher-form) class-size data uses a different method and is excluded.

These were stacked into a district-by-year panel by src/build_enrollment_teachers.py; the dataset’s own SOURCE.md records the build in full.

Caveats worth repeating. num_teachers is a district-wide headcount (all grades, not full-time-equivalents), while k12_enrollment is K-12 only. The derived students-per-teacher ratio is therefore an approximate measure for comparison across districts and over time — not NYSED’s official pupil-teacher ratio or average class size. Charter schools are excluded (they have no district-total row), and a small number of districts reorganized within the window (statewide, not in this comparison group).

Student test results (NYSED grades 3-8 assessments)

To look at student performance, the project uses NYSED’s annual Report Card Database (also from https://data.nysed.gov/downloads.php, distributed as Microsoft Access files). These databases hold the results of New York’s grades 3-8 English Language Arts (ELA) and Mathematics tests — the statewide exams every public-school student takes each spring — reported for each district (and for statewide, county, and “similar-needs” peer groupings) and broken out by student group. Coverage built so far: school years 2015–16 through 2024–25 (ten years). The same databases also contain Regents and other results, which may be added later. The ten annual files were stitched into one district-by-year panel by src/build_assessments.py; full provenance is in data/raw/nysed_report_card/SOURCE.md.

The headline measure is the percentage of students scoring at or above proficiency (performance Level 3 or higher); the data also include the number tested and the average score. Two cautions matter a great deal for reading trends:

  • Spring 2020 is missing and 2021 is unusual. The grades 3-8 tests were cancelled in spring 2020 because of COVID-19, so that year has no data; in spring 2021 only about half of students were tested, so 2021’s numbers are not representative.
  • The tests were redesigned twice, so scores don’t compare across the breaks. New York set new tests and new passing standards in 2018 and again in 2023 (the Next Generation Learning Standards). Results are comparable within each of three periods — 2015–2017, 2018–2022, and 2023–2025 — but a change in the proficiency rate across one of those boundaries reflects the new standard as much as any change in student learning. Comparisons between districts in the same year are unaffected by this and are the most reliable use of the data.

National pupil–teacher ratios (NCES Common Core of Data)

To cross-check the headcount-based ratio above against a full-time-equivalent measure, the project pulls the federal Common Core of Data (CCD) school- district directory — which reports teacher FTE — through the Urban Institute’s Education Data Portal (https://educationdata.urban.org), which mirrors NCES. CCD labels a year by its fall term, so it is shifted to this project’s school-year-ending convention (CCD 2024 → 2024–25).

School district finance (NYS Comptroller)

Per-pupil spending comes from the New York State Comptroller’s (OSC) local-government financial data — annual revenue and expenditure detail, by account code, that every district reports in its Annual Update Document under the state Uniform System of Accounts. Funds are identified by the leading letter of the account code (General, Special Aid, School Lunch, Capital, Debt Service). The current operating spending per pupil measure used in the spending chapter keeps the General, Special Aid, and School Lunch funds and excludes capital outlay, debt service, and transfers between funds, to approximate the U.S. Census / NCES basis for comparing districts. The fiscal year ends June 30 and matches the school-year-ending label (no off-by-one). Dollars are nominal (not adjusted for inflation). Full provenance: data/raw/osc_school_finance/SOURCE.md.

Property Tax Report Card (NYSED)

NYSED posts a statewide Property Tax Report Card workbook each year (https://www.p12.nysed.gov/mgtserv/propertytax/) recording, for every district, the proposed budget, the proposed and prior-year tax levy, the district’s tax levy limit, projected enrollment, and reserve balances — the numbers put before voters each May. These are proposed/budgeted figures (forward- looking), in contrast to the Comptroller’s actual spending. Provenance and the year-by-year download list: data/raw/nysed_property_tax_report_card/SOURCE.md.

School district property taxes (NYS Comptroller)

To show how much property tax each school district raises and how heavily it taxes property, the project uses the Comptroller’s “Table 3” school-district tax workbooks (the same OSC local-government data program as the finance files, at https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/data/real-property-tax-levies-taxable-full-value-and-full-value-tax-rates). For every district and each year 2015 through 2025, they give the district’s tax levy (total property tax the district raises), its taxable full value (the market-value tax base, after equalizing assessed values across towns), and the full-value tax rate — the levy per $1,000 of full value, the standard way to compare tax effort across places with different assessment practices.

A distinctive feature: each district’s figures are broken out by the city or town the district covers — many districts straddle several towns — so the data has both a detail row per district-and-town and a district total. The totals are summed from the parts (and match the Comptroller’s own published totals, where it publishes them, to the dollar). The fiscal year ends June 30 and matches the school-year-ending label, the same as the finance data (verified against the Property Tax Report Card). Districts are joined to the project’s key via a generated crosswalk; 678 of 680 link, the two exceptions being New York City (which the state splits into many borough districts) and one non-operating district. Full provenance, the format-change history, and caveats (including a 2019-file quirk) are in data/raw/osc_school_district_tax_levies/SOURCE.md.

Cambridge CSD annual financial reports

Cambridge’s audited annual financial statements, for fiscal years ended June 30, 2020 through June 30, 2025, were retrieved from the Federal Audit Clearinghouse (https://app.fac.gov/). The clearinghouse was operated by the U.S. Census Bureau through about 2022 and by the U.S. General Services Administration from 2023 onward — which is why the earlier files (FY2020–FY2022) carry a CENSUS tag and bear the title “Financial statements and independent auditor’s report,” while the later files (FY2023–FY2025) carry a GSAFAC tag and the title “Financial report.” They are the same series of district audits.

  • FY2020 (Cambridge Central School District, 2020), FY2021 (Cambridge Central School District, 2021), FY2022 (Cambridge Central School District, 2022)
  • FY2023 (Cambridge Central School District, 2023), FY2024 (Cambridge Central School District, 2024), FY2025 (Cambridge Central School District, 2025)

Candidate sources under consideration

These are not yet used on the website but are likely additions, both from New York’s open-data portal (https://data.ny.gov):

  • State school aid (dataset 9pb8-dg53) — state aid to each school district by year, back to the mid-1990s. Would let the spending chapter separate state-funded from locally-funded dollars.
  • Real property tax rates & levy data by municipality (dataset iq85-sdzs) — tax levies and rates by municipality, useful for putting each district’s school tax levy in the context of total local property taxes.
