6  The property-tax cap

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

New York’s property-tax cap is the most important single rule governing how fast a school district’s tax levy — the total property tax it collects — can grow. This chapter uses NYSED’s Property Tax Report Card, the figures each district files in spring showing the budget it puts before voters, to see how much room Cambridge has under the cap and how the cap binds districts across the state.

It pairs with two other chapters: Taxes, which covers the levy and tax rate Cambridge actually collected; and Contingency budgets, which covers what happens when a budget vote fails.

6.1 How the cap works

In plain terms: a district may raise its tax levy by the lesser of 2% or the inflation rate (CPI) without any special action. To grow the levy faster than that, the budget must win a 60% supermajority at the polls — not the usual simple majority (50% + 1). Each district’s allowable growth for the year, after the state’s formula, is its levy limit.

There is an important escape valve. Certain costs — growth in the pension contribution, spending on capital projects, and some court-ordered payments — can be excluded from the cap. So a district whose proposed levy is above its base levy limit may still be within the cap once these permissible exclusions are applied. Only the amount over the limit after exclusions triggers the 60% requirement. (This is why the Contingency budgets chapter relies on NYSED’s vote-file 60%-flag for actual override outcomes, rather than on a levy-vs-limit calculation alone.)

long = (
    cam.filter(pl.col("year_end") >= 2015)
    .select("year_end", "proposed_m", "limit_m")
    .unpivot(index="year_end", variable_name="measure", value_name="amount")

(
    ggplot(long, aes("year_end", "amount", color="measure"))
    + geom_line(size=1)
    + geom_point(size=1.8)
    + scale_color_manual(values={"Proposed levy": "#C8102E", "Levy limit": "#555555"}, name="")
    + scale_x_continuous(breaks=list(range(2015, 2028)))
    + labs(
        x="School year ending",
        y="Tax levy (millions of dollars)",
        title="Cambridge's proposed tax levy and its levy limit, 2014–15 to 2026–27",
    )
    + cc.theme_ccs()
    + theme(figure_size=(9, 4.5), legend_position="top")
)

For more than a decade Cambridge proposed a levy almost exactly at its levy limit — right up against the cap, but not over it. For 2026–27 the proposed levy breaks sharply above the limit.

6.3 Year by year

Code 
tbl = (
    cam.filter(pl.col("year_end") >= 2015)
    .with_columns(
        (pl.col("levy_gap") / pl.col("tax_levy_limit_wo_exclusions") * 100).round(1).alias("over_limit_pct"),
    )
    .select(
        pl.col("year_end").cast(pl.Utf8).alias("School year ending"),
        pl.col("proposed_m").round(2).alias("Proposed levy ($M)"),
        pl.col("limit_m").round(2).alias("Levy limit ($M)"),
        pl.col("over_limit_pct").alias("Over the limit (%)"),
        pl.col("exclusions_m").round(2).alias("Exclusions ($M)"),
        pl.col("tax_levy_pct_change").round(1).alias("Levy change (%)"),
    )
    .rename({"School year ending": "School year ending"})
)

(
    GT(tbl, rowname_col="School year ending")
    .tab_header(
        title="Cambridge vs. its tax-levy limit",
        subtitle="Proposed levy, the cap, and the gap, 2014–15 to 2026–27",
    )
    .fmt_number(columns=["Proposed levy ($M)", "Levy limit ($M)", "Exclusions ($M)"], decimals=2)
    .fmt_number(columns="Over the limit (%)", decimals=1)
    .fmt_number(columns="Levy change (%)", decimals=1)
    .tab_style(
        style=[style.fill(color="#FCEFB4"), style.text(weight="bold")],
        locations=loc.body(rows=pl.col("School year ending") == "2027"),
    )
    .tab_source_note(
        md(
            "**Source:** NYSED Property Tax Report Card. Gap = proposed levy − levy "
            "limit (both excluding permissible exclusions); positive = over the cap. "
            "Year = school-year-ending (2027 = 2026–27)."
        )
    )
)

Cambridge sat at or just under its levy limit in every year from 2014–15 through 2025–26 — proposing, in most years, a levy equal to the maximum allowed under the cap. For 2026–27 it proposed a levy about $1.1 million (≈12%) over the limit; even after roughly $0.6 million of permissible exclusions, roughly $0.5 million remains over the cap, which would require a 60% supermajority to pass.

6.4 How binding is the cap statewide?

Code 
statewide = (
    cc.load_ptrc()
    .with_columns(
        (pl.col("proposed_tax_levy_wo_exclusions")
         - pl.col("tax_levy_limit_wo_exclusions")
         - pl.col("permissible_exclusions")).alias("over_after_excl"),
    )
    .group_by("year_end")
    .agg((pl.col("over_after_excl") > 0).sum().alias("over_cap"))
    .sort("year_end")
    .filter(pl.col("year_end") >= 2015)
)

(
    ggplot(statewide, aes("year_end", "over_cap"))
    + geom_col(fill="#2C6E9B", width=0.7)
    + scale_x_continuous(breaks=list(range(2015, 2028)))
    + labs(
        x="School year ending",
        y="Districts over the cap",
        title="Districts proposing above the tax cap, 2014–15 to 2026–27",
    )
    + cc.theme_ccs()
    + theme(figure_size=(9, 4))
)

Each year only a relative handful of New York’s roughly 670 districts propose a levy above their cap even after exclusions — the budgets that must clear the 60% bar. The count runs from the low single digits to the low twenties in a given year, and is the pool from which override failures (and therefore contingency budgets) can follow. What happens next — pass or fail — is the subject of the Contingency budgets chapter.

6.5 A note on reserves

A district facing the cap can draw on fund balance — its reserves — to ease the squeeze. Cambridge reports an unrestricted fund balance of about 3–4% of its budget across these years, and in some years uses a tax-cap reserve. The role of reserves and fund balance in Cambridge’s finances is taken up in the reserves chapter.

## How the cap works

## Cambridge's proposed levy vs. its levy limit

```{python}
#| label: setup
#| include: false
import os
import sys

sys.path.insert(0, os.getcwd())

import polars as pl
from plotnine import (
    aes, geom_col, geom_line, geom_point, ggplot, labs,
    scale_color_manual, scale_x_continuous, theme,
)
from great_tables import GT, loc, md, style

import comparisons as cc

# Consistent over/under gap (proposed - limit; +over / -under). We compute this
# ourselves because NYSED flips the sign of the stored levy_vs_limit field at 2016.
ptrc = cc.ptrc_for(cc.GRAPH_GROUP).with_columns(
    (pl.col("proposed_tax_levy_wo_exclusions") - pl.col("tax_levy_limit_wo_exclusions")).alias("levy_gap"),
    (pl.col("proposed_tax_levy_wo_exclusions") / 1e6).alias("proposed_m"),
    (pl.col("tax_levy_limit_wo_exclusions") / 1e6).alias("limit_m"),
    (pl.col("permissible_exclusions") / 1e6).alias("exclusions_m"),
)
cam = ptrc.filter(pl.col("district") == cc.FOCUS)
```
