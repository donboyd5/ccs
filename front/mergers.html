Cambridge, like many small rural districts in New York, is shrinking. Enrollment is falling and the population is aging (see Demographics), which puts steady pressure on spending, taxes, and reserves (see those chapters). One structural response New York explicitly allows is merger — combining two or more school districts into one. This chapter explains what merger means in New York, the financial incentive the state offers for it, what has actually happened when New York districts have merged, and where Cambridge sits among its neighbors.

Whether merger is right for Cambridge is a decision for the community and its school board. What follows is the mechanics and the evidence.

10.1 What “merger” means in New York

New York’s term is reorganization — the act of creating a new school district where one or more existed before. State Education Law §314 keeps a running “Master Plan for School District Reorganization” (first drawn up in 1942) so district boundaries can change as communities’ needs and tax bases shift.

There are three main forms:

  • Centralization — the merging districts dissolve and a new school district is created covering all of their territory. (This is what most people mean by “merger.”)
  • Annexation — one district dissolves and is absorbed into a neighboring Central or Union Free district; no new district is created.
  • Annexation by a city school district — a district outside a city is absorbed into the city district, which becomes an “Enlarged City School District.”

A district can also “tuition” students — contract with a neighbor to educate a grade level — but that is not a merger: no new district is formed, and no reorganization incentive aid is paid.

10.2 How a merger happens

The process takes roughly a year and has several built-in public checkpoints (NYSED, School District Reorganization: A Primer, July 2024):

  1. The boards of the affected districts hold joint meetings and commission a feasibility study (enrollment, staffing, programs, transportation, and the fiscal picture — state aid, spending, and the local tax impact). A state Local Government Efficiency Grant can offset the study’s cost.
  2. The boards hold public information meetings.
  3. Each district holds a non-binding “straw vote” to gauge support.
  4. The BOCES District Superintendent reports the straw-vote result to the Commissioner of Education; a letter from the Superintendent in support starts the formal (statutory) process.
  5. The Commissioner issues an order; the statutory vote follows; the earliest a new district can begin operating is July 1.

10.3 Two routes, two vote thresholds

The single most important practical difference between centralization and annexation is how the vote works:

  • Centralization requires eligible voters in each district to petition for a vote, and a majority in favor at every voting location is needed to form the new district. In other words, both communities must say yes.
  • Annexation is different: the Commissioner’s order is final after 60 days unless residents petition for a vote. A vote is not legally required, and a majority in favor at any one voting district finalizes the annexation.

So annexation is the lower-bar path — it can be carried by a single district’s yes vote, or even take effect without a vote. That asymmetry shapes which route real merger discussions tend to take.

10.4 The financial incentive

The state pushes districts toward merger with a large but temporary aid package:

  • Reorganization Incentive Operating Aid (RIOA) pays the merged district an extra 40% of its combined Foundation Aid for the first five years, then drops by four percentage points a year, ending after 14 years. Since 2024-25, that percentage is applied to the merged district’s Foundation Aid at the effective date of the merger; before that it used a frozen 2006-07 base, so the 2024 change roughly tripled the incentive for a district merging today. (A long-dormant statutory limit can reduce the amount for some districts; NYSED advises districts to check.)
  • Reorganization Incentive Building Aid lifts a district’s building-aid ratio by 30 percentage points (capped at 95%) for new or renovated space, available for 10 years.

For scale, here is what the 14-year RIOA stream looks like applied to Cambridge’s own Foundation Aid ($9.55 million in 2025-26) as an illustrative base. The real amount would be 40% of the combined Foundation Aid of whatever districts merged, so this is a lower bound for a Cambridge-centered merger.

Two real New York mergers show what that money — and its limits — look like in practice (NYSSBA On Board, April 2025):

  • Central Valley CSD (Ilion + Mohawk, Herkimer County, 2013) is on track to receive about $42 million in incentive aid over its 14 years. It used the money to expand offerings (16 AP courses plus 25-30 college-credit courses), add sports and extracurriculars, bolster reserves, and hold down the tax levy. But it is now in year 12 of 14 — the incentive is almost gone, and district leaders describe hard decisions ahead. (Central Valley got 73.5% of its funding from state aid in 2022-23.)
  • Boquet Valley CSD (Elizabethtown-Lewis + Westport, Essex County, 2019) receives $6 million over 15 years under the old formula — it would have gotten $18 million under the current one. It expanded electives and AP options, but still operates in two aging buildings; a capital project to consolidate onto one campus was defeated by voters, and the community still wrangles over the school’s name and colors.

A retired BOCES district superintendent, Robert Guiffreda, has suggested merged districts split incentive aid roughly into thirds — tax relief, enhanced programming, and capital needs (more if a new building is required).

10.5 Mergers are rare

For all the incentive, very few New York districts actually merge. The two above are among the only centralizations in recent decades. Far more common are studies that go nowhere: roughly 30 districts have studied merger since 2010 without implementing one (per the state school business officials’ association), and failed votes dot the record — Scio and Wellsville (2010), Lake Pleasant and Wells (2011). Loss of local identity — team nicknames, mascots, rivalries — is one of the most frequently cited reasons a merger vote fails.

10.6 What the research says

The broader research on district consolidation (e.g., a review of the research on district and school consolidation) points in a consistent direction on money, a less settled one on academics:

  • Cost savings are real but concentrated among the smallest districts. Combining two small districts can lower per-pupil costs meaningfully; the savings shrink as the districts involved get larger, and can be eaten back by longer bus rides and higher transportation costs.
  • Academic effects are mixed. Larger districts can offer more courses and programs (as Central Valley and Boquet Valley found), but evidence that consolidation by itself raises student achievement is weak.
  • The trade-offs are local: longer travel times, less local control, staff disruption, and the loss of a community institution.

In short, the financial case is strongest precisely for the kind of small rural districts that surround Cambridge.

10.7 Where Cambridge stands

Cambridge is a mid-size district in a county of small ones. The table below places it among its Washington County neighbors (plus Schuylerville and Hoosick Falls, just across the county line). Cambridge’s 741 students and $9.5 million in Foundation Aid sit in the middle of the pack; five neighbors are smaller by enrollment (Hartford, Fort Edward, Fort Ann, Argyle, Salem) — and the research above finds consolidation’s per-pupil savings are largest precisely when the districts combining are small.

group = {**cc.WASHINGTON_K12, "52170104": "Schuylerville", "49050106": "Hoosick Falls"}

enr = (cc.load_panel()
       .filter((pl.col("district_cd").is_in(list(group))) & (pl.col("year_end") == 2025))
       .select(pl.col("district_cd").replace_strict(group).alias("district"),
               "district_cd", "k12_enrollment"))

fa = (state_aid
      .filter((pl.col("aid_category") == "Foundation Aid") & (pl.col("year_end") == 2026))
      .select(pl.col("nysed_district_cd").alias("district_cd"),
              pl.col("amount").alias("foundation_aid")))

tbl = (enr.join(fa, on="district_cd", how="left")
       .with_columns((pl.col("foundation_aid") / pl.col("k12_enrollment")).alias("fa_per_pupil"))

```{python}
import os, sys
from pathlib import Path
sys.path.insert(0, os.getcwd())
import polars as pl
from plotnine import (
    aes, geom_col, geom_text, ggplot, labs, scale_x_continuous, scale_y_continuous,
    theme,
)
from great_tables import GT, loc, md, style
import comparisons as cc

def _find_repo_root() -> Path:
    cwd = Path.cwd()
    for base in [cwd, *cwd.parents][:5]:
        if (base / "data" / "crosswalks").is_dir():
            return base
    raise FileNotFoundError("Could not find repo root")

REPO_ROOT = _find_repo_root()
PROCESSED = REPO_ROOT / "data" / "processed"

state_aid = pl.read_parquet(PROCESSED / "state_aid.parquet")

# Cambridge's latest Foundation Aid (used as an illustrative RIOA base).
cam_fa = (state_aid.filter((pl.col("district") == "Cambridge") &
                           (pl.col("aid_category") == "Foundation Aid"))
          .sort("year_end").select(pl.col("amount").last()).item())

# RIOA percentage schedule: 40% for 5 years, then -4 pts/yr for 9 years.
RIOA_PCT = [40]*5 + [36, 32, 28, 24, 20, 16, 12, 8, 4]
rioa = pl.DataFrame({"year": list(range(1, 15)), "pct": RIOA_PCT})
rioa = rioa.with_columns((pl.col("pct") / 100 * cam_fa).alias("aid"))
rioa_total = rioa["aid"].sum()
```
