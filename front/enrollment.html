Cambridge has fewer students than it used to — about 124 fewer K-12 students in 2024–25 than in 2015–16, a decline of roughly 14%.

Fewer students mean:

  • This matters because fewer students mean less state aid, a smaller tax base to spread costs across, and tough choices about staffing and programs. Enrollment is the starting point for everything that follows: staffing levels, spending per pupil, and the district’s fiscal health.

The figure below shows how Cambridge’s K-12 enrollment has changed since 2015–16, alongside nearby districts for context.

Code 
g = (
    cc.panel_for(cc.GRAPH_GROUP, since=2016)
    .select("district", "year_end", "k12_enrollment")
    .drop_nulls()
)
cambridge = g.filter(pl.col("district") == cc.FOCUS)

(
    ggplot(g, aes("year_end", "k12_enrollment", color="district"))
    + geom_line(size=1)
    + geom_point(size=1.8)
    + geom_line(cambridge, size=2.4)  # emphasize the focus district
    + scale_color_manual(
        values=cc.PALETTE, name="", limits=cc.district_order(cc.GRAPH_GROUP)
    )
    + scale_x_continuous(breaks=list(range(2016, 2026)))
    + scale_y_continuous(limits=(450, 1400), breaks=list(range(500, 1401, 200)))
    + labs(
        x="School year ending",
        y="K–12 enrollment",
        title="K-12 enrollment, 2015–16 to 2024–25",
    )
    + cc.theme_ccs()
)

The districts differ greatly in size, which is why the chart above clips Schuylerville (1,645 students in 2016) off the top while bunching Salem (512) near the bottom. Setting each district’s 2016 enrollment equal to 100 puts them on one shared scale, so the lines compare rates of change rather than size:

g = (
    cc.panel_for(cc.GRAPH_GROUP, since=2016)
    .select("district", "year_end", "k12_enrollment")

# set each district's 2016 enrollment equal to 100
base = (
    g.filter(pl.col("year_end") == 2016)

Cambridge’s enrollment decline is real, but it is not alone. The trend is regional. Schuylerville and Greenwich have seen larger absolute losses (327 and 219 students respectively), while Hoosick Falls and Salem have held steadier. The pandemic year 2020–21 shows a sharp dip across districts, followed by partial recoveries that did not fully return to pre-pandemic levels.

The table below shows where each district stood in 2015–16 and 2024–25, and how much they gained or lost over the decade.

Code 
g = cc.panel_for(cc.GRAPH_GROUP, since=2016).filter(
    pl.col("year_end").is_in([2016, 2025])
)

wide = (
    g.with_columns(
        pl.when(pl.col("region") == "Washington County").then(0).otherwise(1).alias("_rank")
    )
    .pivot(values="k12_enrollment", index=["_rank", "region", "district"], on="year_end")
    .sort(["_rank", "district"])
    .drop("_rank")
)

wide = wide.with_columns(
    (pl.col("2025") - pl.col("2016")).alias("change"),
    ((pl.col("2025") - pl.col("2016")) / pl.col("2016") * 100).alias("pct_change"),
)

(
    GT(wide, rowname_col="district", groupname_col="region")
    .tab_header(
        title="K-12 enrollment change",
        subtitle="School-year-ending 2016 (2015–16) and 2025 (2024–25), with change"
    )
    .fmt_number(columns=["2016", "2025", "change"], decimals=0)
    .fmt_number(columns="pct_change", decimals=1, force_sign=True)
    .tab_spanner(label="School year ending", columns=["2016", "2025"])
    .cols_label(pct_change=md("% change"))
    .tab_style(
        style=[style.fill(color="#FCEFB4"), style.text(weight="bold")],
        locations=[
            loc.body(rows=pl.col("district") == cc.FOCUS),
            loc.stub(rows=pl.col("district") == cc.FOCUS),
        ],
    )
    .tab_source_note(
        md(
            "**Source:** NYSED BEDS Day enrollment database. K-12 enrollment as of "
            "the first Wednesday of October."
        )
    )
)

Cambridge lost 124 students (−14%) over the decade, the second-largest decline among the five districts after Schuylerville’s 327-student loss (−20%). Greenwich lost 219 students (−21%). Salem and Hoosick Falls held closer to flat.

2.1 What this means

Enrollment decline sets the context for everything else in Cambridge’s budget:

  • State aid is tied to students. Fewer students mean less foundation aid from New York State, unless the state adjusts its formulas.
  • Fixed costs don’t shrink. A building, a bus route, a principal — these costs don’t automatically fall when enrollment does. Spreading fixed costs over fewer students is part of why Cambridge’s spending per pupil ranks high (see the Spending chapter).
  • Staffing choices get harder. Cambridge now has the fewest students per teacher of the comparison group (see the Class size chapter) — a choice that preserves small classes but adds pressure at budget time.

The enrollment trend is a regional challenge, not unique to Cambridge. But the scale of the decline — and the decisions it forces about what to preserve — is what Cambridge’s community must grapple with.
