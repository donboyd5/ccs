8  Contingency budgets

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Every May, each New York school district puts its proposed budget to a public vote. If a budget is defeated — fewer than half of voters say yes — the district gets a second chance: it may hold a re-vote in June. If the budget fails again, or the district decides not to try again, the district must operate the next year on a contingency budget — a pared-back spending plan set by state rules, with no new programs and tight limits on spending growth.

One rule shapes most of what follows. New York’s property-tax cap says a district may raise its tax levy by only a small amount each year without special permission. A district that wants to go above the cap must win a 60% supermajority at the polls. A budget at or below the cap needs only a simple majority (more than 50%). That higher, 60% bar is why above-cap budgets fail more often — and why the tax cap and contingency budgets are linked.

This chapter uses NYSED’s statewide School District Budget Voting Results to count, year by year, how many districts defeat a budget, hold a re-vote, and end up on a contingency budget — and where Cambridge and its neighbors fit in.

8.1 How often budgets fail

Budget defeats are rare, and most districts that fail once recover. Across the 14 votes from 2012 through 2025, only 198 of roughly 9,400 district-votes ended in a first-vote defeat — about two per 100. Of those 198 defeats, 125 districts (63%) passed their budget on the June re-vote, and 73 (37%) went on to a contingency budget. Defeats cluster in single digits most years, with 2025 (24 defeats) the highest since 2013.

8.3 The 60% threshold

The tax cap’s 60% supermajority rule is the mechanism behind most above-cap defeats. The table below follows the 198 districts that failed a first vote and asks, for their second try: was the budget they put up above the cap (needing 60%) or at or below the cap (needing only a simple majority), and did it pass or send the district to contingency?

Code 
# Cap status of the budget governing the second event: the re-vote's flag if a
# re-vote was held, else the first vote's flag. Null where no 60% flag was
# reported (older files).
cap_outcome = (
    panel.with_columns(pl.coalesce(["above_cap2", "above_cap"]).alias("cap2"))
    .with_columns(
        pl.when(pl.col("cap2").is_null())
        .then(pl.lit("No 60% flag in file"))
        .when(pl.col("cap2"))
        .then(pl.lit("Above cap — 60% required"))
        .otherwise(pl.lit("At/below cap — simple majority"))
        .alias("cap_status")
    )
    .group_by("cap_status", "outcome")
    .len()
    .pivot("outcome", index="cap_status", values="len", aggregate_function="sum")
    .fill_null(0)
)

# Stable row order and tidy outcome columns.
order = ["Above cap — 60% required", "At/below cap — simple majority", "No 60% flag in file"]
cap_outcome = (
    cap_outcome.with_columns(pl.col("cap_status").replace_strict({k: i for i, k in enumerate(order)}).alias("_o"))
    .sort("_o")
    .drop("_o")
)
for c in ["Passed on revote", "Contingency (failed revote)", "Contingency (no revote)"]:
    if c not in cap_outcome.columns:
        cap_outcome = cap_outcome.with_columns(pl.lit(0, dtype=pl.Int64).alias(c))
cap_outcome = cap_outcome.select("cap_status", "Passed on revote",
                                 "Contingency (failed revote)", "Contingency (no revote)")

(
    GT(cap_outcome, rowname_col="cap_status")
    .tab_header(
        title="Second-vote outcomes by tax-cap status",
        subtitle="Districts that failed the first vote, 2012–2025",
    )
    .tab_spanner(label="Passed", columns=["Passed on revote"])
    .tab_spanner(label="Went to contingency", columns=["Contingency (failed revote)", "Contingency (no revote)"])
    .tab_source_note(
        md(
            "**Source:** NYSED statewide budget voting results (computed). Cap "
            "status comes from NYSED's 60%-required flag, the only cap signal these "
            "files carry (they include no levy figures). 'No 60% flag in file' covers "
            "older files that did not report the flag."
        )
    )
)

Even a simple-majority budget can fall: 23 districts that brought an at-or- below-cap budget to a second vote still could not reach 50% and went to contingency. But above-cap budgets, which must clear the higher 60% bar, are the harder case — 39 of them ended on contingency.

8.6 Notes on the data

  • What “year” means. This chapter indexes by the vote’s calendar year (the May 2024 vote is “2024”). The budget adopted at that vote funds the following school year (the May 2024 vote adopts the 2024–25 budget). That is one off from the school-year-ending label used elsewhere in this book.
  • No levy figures. NYSED’s vote files carry no tax-levy data — only a “60% required” flag marking budgets above the cap. Cap status here comes entirely from that flag; the actual levy percentages live in a different source (the OSC / tax report-card data used in the Taxes chapter).
  • Re-vote linking is best-effort. A first vote and its re-vote are matched by district name within the year. NYSED spells district names inconsistently across files (and used all-caps in the older files), so a handful of re-votes each year do not link to a first-vote record; counts of re-vote outcomes are therefore approximate for the older years and reliable from about 2020 on.
  • Pre-2012. The tax cap took effect in 2012, so the above/below-cap split is undefined before then; earlier years are omitted.
  • Validation. The computed first-vote defeat counts match NYSED’s own published statewide totals exactly for 2021–2025 (and within one for the anomalous 2020).

# Contingency budgets

Every May, each New York school district puts its proposed budget to a public
vote. If a budget is **defeated** — fewer than half of voters say yes — the
district gets a second chance: it may hold a **re-vote** in June. If the budget
fails again, or the district decides not to try again, the district must operate
the next year on a **contingency budget** — a pared-back spending plan set by
state rules, with no new programs and tight limits on spending growth.

One rule shapes most of what follows. New York's property-tax cap says a district
may raise its tax levy by only a small amount each year without special
permission. A district that wants to go **above the cap** must win a 60%
supermajority at the polls. A budget **at or below the cap** needs only a simple
majority (more than 50%). That higher, 60% bar is why above-cap budgets fail more
often — and why the tax cap and contingency budgets are linked.

This chapter uses NYSED's statewide *School District Budget Voting Results* to
count, year by year, how many districts defeat a budget, hold a re-vote, and end
up on a contingency budget — and where Cambridge and its neighbors fit in.

## How often budgets fail

```{python}
#| label: setup
#| include: false
import os
import sys

sys.path.insert(0, os.getcwd())

import polars as pl
from plotnine import (
    aes, geom_col, ggplot, labs, position_dodge, scale_fill_manual,
    scale_x_continuous, scale_y_continuous, theme,
)
from great_tables import GT, loc, md, style

import comparisons as cc

# --- classify each first vote and, where there was one, the re-vote ---
bv = cc.load_budget_votes()


def _passed(yes, no, above_cap):
    """A budget passes at >=60% yes if it was above the cap, else >50%."""
    share = yes / (yes + no)
    return pl.when(above_cap).then(share >= 0.60).otherwise(share > 0.50)


first = (
    bv.filter(pl.col("kind") == "vote")
    .with_columns(
        (pl.col("yes") / (pl.col("yes") + pl.col("no"))).alias("share1"),
        _passed(pl.col("yes"), pl.col("no"), pl.col("above_cap")).alias("passed1"),
    )
)
second = (
    bv.filter(pl.col("kind") == "revote")
    .with_columns(
        (pl.col("yes") / (pl.col("yes") + pl.col("no"))).alias("share2"),
        _passed(pl.col("yes"), pl.col("no"), pl.col("above_cap")).alias("passed2"),
    )
    .select("year", "district_key", pl.col("above_cap").alias("above_cap2"),
            "share2", "passed2")
)

# Start from districts that FAILED the first vote, and attach their re-vote.
panel = (
    first.filter(pl.col("passed1") == False)  # noqa: E712
    .join(second, on=["year", "district_key"], how="left")
    .with_columns(pl.col("passed2").is_not_null().alias("revote_held"))
    .with_columns(
        pl.when(pl.col("revote_held") & (pl.col("passed2") == True))  # noqa: E712
        .then(pl.lit("Passed on revote"))
        .when(pl.col("revote_held") & (pl.col("passed2") == False))  # noqa: E712
        .then(pl.lit("Contingency (failed revote)"))
        .when(~pl.col("revote_held"))
        .then(pl.lit("Contingency (no revote)"))
        .otherwise(pl.lit("Unresolved"))
        .alias("outcome")
    )
)
```

ended in a first-vote defeat — about two per 100. Of those 198 defeats, **125
districts (63%) passed their budget on the June re-vote**, and **73 (37%) went on
to a contingency budget**. Defeats cluster in single digits most years, with 2025
(24 defeats) the highest since 2013.

  for the **June re-vote**, where 71% voted yes, and passed.

No other Washington County district in this book's comparison group (Greenwich,
Granville, Hudson Falls, Whitehall, and the rest) defeated a budget in any year on
record.

## Who ends up on contingency

The districts that reach a contingency budget are a small, rotating set —
statewide, **73 districts across 14 years**, never more than a handful in one
year. A few recur: **East Ramapo** (Rockland County), the state's most fiscally
troubled large district, went to contingency in both 2020 and 2022. The table
below lists every district that ended on contingency in the most recent six vote
years.

```{python}
#| label: tbl-contingency-recent
#| tbl-cap: "Districts that operated on a contingency budget, 2020–2025, with the reason."
recent_cont = (
    panel.filter((pl.col("year") >= 2020) & pl.col("outcome").str.starts_with("Contingency"))
    .select(pl.col("district").alias("District"),
            pl.col("year").cast(pl.Utf8).alias("Vote year"),
            pl.col("outcome").alias("How it happened"))
    .sort("Vote year", "District")
)
(
    GT(recent_cont, rowname_col="District")
    .tab_header(title="Districts on a contingency budget, 2020–2025")
    .cols_label(**{"Vote year": "Vote year", "How it happened": "How it happened"})
    .tab_options(row_striping_include_table_body=True)
    .tab_source_note(
        md(
            '**Source:** NYSED statewide budget voting results (computed). "Failed '
            'revote" = held a June re-vote and lost; "no revote" = adopted contingency '

    )
)
```

## Notes on the data

- **What "year" means.** This chapter indexes by the **vote's calendar year** (the
  May 2024 vote is "2024"). The budget adopted at that vote funds the **following**
  school year (the May 2024 vote adopts the 2024–25 budget). That is one off from
  the school-year-ending label used elsewhere in this book.
- **No levy figures.** NYSED's vote files carry **no tax-levy data** — only a "60%
  required" flag marking budgets above the cap. Cap status here comes entirely from
  that flag; the actual levy percentages live in a different source (the OSC / tax
  report-card data used in the [Taxes](taxes.qmd) chapter).
- **Re-vote linking is best-effort.** A first vote and its re-vote are matched by
  district name within the year. NYSED spells district names inconsistently across
  files (and used all-caps in the older files), so a handful of re-votes each year
  do not link to a first-vote record; counts of re-vote outcomes are therefore
  approximate for the older years and reliable from about 2020 on.
- **Pre-2012.** The tax cap took effect in 2012, so the above/below-cap split is
  undefined before then; earlier years are omitted.
- **Validation.** The computed first-vote defeat counts match NYSED's own published
  statewide totals exactly for 2021–2025 (and within one for the anomalous 2020).
