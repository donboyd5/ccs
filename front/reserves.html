7  Reserves and fund balance

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

A school district’s reserves and fund balance are its savings — money set aside from prior years that is not yet spent. They are a buffer for cash-flow gaps, a cushion against unexpected costs, and a way to save for planned future expenses. The size of a district’s reserves is also one of the clearest signs of its fiscal health: too little leaves it exposed; the state limits how much of the flexible kind a district may hold.

This chapter explains the different kinds of reserves a New York school district keeps, the legal cap on the flexible part, how districts across the state sit relative to that cap, and how Cambridge’s own reserves have changed. It draws on the official chart of accounts (kept in this repo’s docs/ folder), NYSED’s Property Tax Report Card, OSC school-district finance data, and Cambridge’s audited annual financial reports.

7.1 Two kinds of “reserves”: reserve funds and fund balance

The word “reserves” gets used two ways, and the distinction matters.

Reserve funds are accounts created under specific state laws, each for a specific purpose. The money in them is restricted — it can only be used for that purpose. A New York school district’s main reserve funds are:

Reserve fund Account code Legal basis Purpose
Capital reserve A878 Education Law §3651 Cost of items for which bonds may be issued (requires voter approval)
Tax certiorari reserve A864 General Municipal Law §3651.1-a Property-tax refund judgments (mandatory; capped at anticipated claims)
Retirement contribution reserve (ERS / TRS) A827 / A828 General Municipal Law §6-r Pension contributions to the state retirement systems
Employee benefit accrued liability reserve A867 General Municipal Law §6-p Accrued employee benefits payable on termination
Insurance reserve A863 General Municipal Law §6-m Liability, property, and casualty deductibles/losses
Repair reserve A878-type General Municipal Law §6-d Non-recurring repairs to capital improvements
Unemployment insurance reserve A815 — Unemployment-insurance claims
Workers’ compensation reserve A814 — Workers’-compensation claims
Reserve for debt A884 — Debt-service requirements

Fund balance is the broader accounting term for the net resources of a fund after liabilities. The General Fund’s fund balance is classified into:

  • Restricted — the reserve funds above (each legally earmarked).
  • Committed — set aside by board action for a specific purpose.
  • Assigned — intended for a particular use (e.g., appropriated into the next year’s budget, or unappropriated).
  • Unassigned — the residual, general-purpose balance with no specific designation. This is the flexible “rainy-day” money.

So the relationship is: reserve funds are the restricted portion of fund balance. A district can hold large amounts in restricted reserves and still have little unassigned balance — and it is the unassigned balance that the law caps.

7.2 The 4% cap

New York’s property-tax-cap law (Real Property Tax Law §2-c / Education Law §2023-a) limits a school district’s unassigned (unexpended-surplus) fund balance to 4% of the prior year’s budget. This is the measure NYSED reports in the Property Tax Report Card as fund_balance_unrestricted_pct_of_budget.

The cap applies only to the unassigned balance, not to the restricted reserve funds. That is why a district with substantial capital, retirement, or tax- certiorari reserves is not “over the cap” — those are restricted for their purposes. The 4% rule constrains only the flexible money a district can keep on hand.1

7.3 Why districts build specific reserves

Each reserve fund exists to set aside money for a specific, often lumpy or unpredictable cost the district is legally required to pay — smoothing a bill that would otherwise hit the budget all at once. The clearest example is the retirement reserve. A district’s pension cost depends on an annually-set employer contribution rate that swings widely: NYSTRS’s teachers’-system rate has ranged from roughly 6% to 17.5% of payroll since 2014-15 (it was 16.25% for 2023-24, for example).2 By contributing to the reserve in lower-rate years, a district builds a cushion it can draw on when the rate spikes — instead of cutting programs or sharply raising taxes that year. The same logic runs through the other reserves: each earmarks savings for a cost that is mandatory, large, and hard to time — tax-refund judgments, insurance or workers’-comp claims, capital projects.

A second reason matters under the tax cap: restricted reserve funds are not subject to the 4% unassigned-fund-balance limit. Building reserves is therefore the main way a district holds savings beyond that 4% flexible ceiling — the trade-off being that the money is locked to its specific purpose.

7.4 Using reserves for budget relief

A district spends down a reserve by appropriating it — booking a planned draw as a revenue source in the coming year’s budget. When, say, $X is appropriated from the TRS reserve toward that year’s pension bill, the budget needs $X less from taxes and state aid for the TRS line, freeing room for other spending, for tax relief, or to rebuild other reserves. Cambridge’s board did this for 2025-26, appropriating $625,000 from the capital reserve and $200,119 from the employee-benefit reserve.

Two points keep this honest:

  • It is not new money. The reserve was funded from prior years’ taxes, so drawing it shifts cost backward and leaves less cushion for the next shock — it smooths timing; it does not create resources.
  • It works within the cap. Using reserves as a revenue source lowers the levy the budget requires — a key lever for staying under the tax cap, though leaning on it too hard erodes the buffer.

Reserve funds can be spent only for their statutory purpose — a TRS reserve cannot buy a roof, and a capital reserve cannot pay a pension bill. Several carry extra rules (from the chart of accounts and Cambridge’s audited reports):

  • Capital reserve (Education Law §3651): both establishing it and every expenditure from it require voter approval by proposition.
  • Tax certiorari reserve (General Municipal Law §3651.1-a): mandatory to fund; the balance may not exceed anticipated judgments and claims, and any unused amount must be returned to the General Fund by the first day of the fourth fiscal year after deposit. (Cambridge was flagged in 2024-25 for over-funding this reserve; it was corrected.)
  • Retirement reserve (General Municipal Law §6-r): a board may contribute up to 2% of covered salaries per year, with the TRS sub-fund capped at 10% of covered salaries.
  • Employee benefit reserve (General Municipal Law §6-p): established by board vote; for accrued benefits payable on termination.

The unassigned balance, by contrast, has no purpose restriction — it is the flexible money — which is precisely why the state caps it at 4%.

Cambridge’s reserves have grown and diversified over the decade:

  • The total General Fund balance roughly doubled, from about $2.5 million in 2012–13 to $5.2 million in 2024–25.
  • The district added reserves it previously did not hold — a TRS (teachers’) retirement reserve from 2020–21, an employee-benefit accrued liability reserve, and a substantial capital reserve (~$1.1 million) from 2021–22.
  • The ERS retirement reserve roughly doubled (to ~$884,000 in 2024–25); the reserve for debt was drawn down to zero as debt retired.
  • The unassigned, 4%-capped balance — the flexible part — has been about $1.0–1.2 million in recent years, sitting at the cap.

```{python}
    + geom_line(cam, aes("year_end", "fund_balance_unrestricted_pct_of_budget"),
                color="#C8102E", size=1.6)
    + geom_point(cam, aes("year_end", "fund_balance_unrestricted_pct_of_budget"),
                 color="#C8102E", size=1.8)
    + scale_x_continuous(breaks=list(range(2015, 2028)))
    + scale_y_continuous(breaks=list(range(0, 11, 2)))
    + labs(
        x="School year ending",
        y="Unassigned fund balance (% of budget)",
        title="District unassigned fund balance vs. the 4% cap, 2014–15 to 2026–27",
    )
    + cc.theme_ccs()
    + theme(figure_size=(9, 4.5))
)
```

The **median district reports exactly 4% every year** — the cap binds, and
districts keep the maximum flexible balance the law allows. A meaningful tail
sits above 4% in some years (the top of the band reaches about 6% in the early
2020s), and roughly **a quarter to a third of districts report over 4%** in a
given year. **Cambridge tracks right at the cap** (about 3–4.6%), a typical
position.

## Total reserves vs. other districts

The 4% chart above covers only the *unassigned* balance. A district's **total**
fund balance — the restricted reserve funds plus assigned and unassigned balance —
is a fuller measure of its savings. As a share of the budget, Cambridge sits
**below** the typical New York district.

```{python}
#| label: fig-total-fb-comparative
#| fig-cap: "Total General Fund fund balance (restricted reserve funds + assigned + unassigned) as a percent of each district's budget, school years ending 2015–2027. Shaded band = middle 50% of districts; solid line = median; Cambridge is the red line. Source: NYSED Property Tax Report Card."
tfb = (
    pl.read_parquet(PTRC_PATH)
    .with_columns(
        (pl.col("fund_balance_restricted").fill_null(0)
         + pl.col("fund_balance_assigned_appropriated").fill_null(0)
         + pl.col("fund_balance_unrestricted").fill_null(0)).alias("total_fb"),
    )
    .filter(pl.col("proposed_spending") > 0)
    .with_columns((pl.col("total_fb") / pl.col("proposed_spending") * 100).alias("fb_pct"))
)
band2 = (
    tfb.group_by("year_end").agg(
        pl.col("fb_pct").quantile(0.25).alias("p25"),
        pl.col("fb_pct").median().alias("median"),
        pl.col("fb_pct").quantile(0.75).alias("p75"),
    ).sort("year_end")
)
cam2 = (
    tfb.filter(pl.col("nysed_district_cd") == "64161004")
    .select("year_end", "fb_pct").sort("year_end")
)

(
    ggplot()
    + geom_ribbon(band2, aes("year_end", ymax="p75", ymin="p25"), fill="#C8D9E6", alpha=0.8)
    + geom_line(band2, aes("year_end", "median"), color="#2C6E9B", size=1)
    + geom_line(cam2, aes("year_end", "fb_pct"), color="#C8102E", size=1.6)
    + geom_point(cam2, aes("year_end", "fb_pct"), color="#C8102E", size=1.8)
    + scale_x_continuous(breaks=list(range(2015, 2028)))
    + scale_y_continuous(breaks=list(range(0, 61, 10)))
    + labs(
        x="School year ending",
        y="Total fund balance (% of budget)",
        title="Total fund balance vs. other districts, 2014–15 to 2026–27",
    )
    + cc.theme_ccs()
    + theme(figure_size=(9, 4.5))
)
```

The typical New York district holds total fund balance worth **about a quarter
of its budget** (the median rose from ~19% in 2014–15 to ~26% by the mid-2020s).
Districts broadly built up savings over the period. **Cambridge sits below that
median** — about 11% of budget in 2014–15, rising to ~19% by 2024–25, around the
**27th percentile statewide**. Cambridge's reserves grew *faster* than the typical
district's over the decade, but from a thinner base, so they remain **comparatively
modest**.

## Cambridge's reserves by type, over time

```{python}
#| label: tbl-cambridge-reserves
#| tbl-cap: "Cambridge Central School District reserve fund balances by type, school years ending 2013–2025, in thousands of dollars. Source: NYS Comptroller school-district finance data (ST-3 account codes A8xx). Blank = reserve not held that year."
fin = pl.read_parquet(CAM_FINANCE_PATH)
years = list(range(2013, 2026))


def _amt(code: str, year: int):
    """End-of-year balance ($, or None) for one account code in one year."""
    s = fin.filter((pl.col("ACCOUNT_CODE") == code) & (pl.col("CALENDAR_YEAR") == year)).select("AMOUNT").to_series()
    return s.first() if s.len() else None


rows = []
for code, label in RESERVE_CODES.items():
    rows.append({"Reserve": label, **{str(y): (_amt(code, y) or 0) / 1000 for y in years}})

res_df = pl.DataFrame(rows)
res_df = pl.concat([
    res_df,
    pl.DataFrame([{"Reserve": "Total restricted reserves",
                   **{str(y): sum(filter(None, (_amt(c, y) for c in RESERVE_CODES))) / 1000
                      for y in years}}]),
    pl.DataFrame([{"Reserve": "Unassigned (4%-capped)",
                   **{str(y): (_amt("A917", y) or 0) / 1000 for y in years}}]),
    pl.DataFrame([{"Reserve": "Total General Fund balance",
                   **{str(y): (_amt("A8029", y) or 0) / 1000 for y in years}}]),
], how="vertical")

year_cols = [str(y) for y in years]
bold_rows = pl.col("Reserve").is_in(["Total restricted reserves", "Total General Fund balance"])

(
    GT(res_df, rowname_col="Reserve")
    .tab_header(
        title="Cambridge's reserve funds by type",
        subtitle="School years ending 2013 (2012–13) through 2025 (2024–25), $ thousands",
    )
    .fmt_number(columns=year_cols, decimals=0)
    .tab_style(
        style=[style.text(weight="bold"), style.fill(color="#FCEFB4")],
        locations=loc.body(rows=bold_rows),
    )
    .tab_source_note(
        md(
            "**Source:** NYS Comptroller local-government finance data (ST-3), account "
            "codes A814–A884 (reserve funds), A917 (unassigned), A8029 (total General "
            "Fund fund balance, end of year). Year = school-year-ending (2025 = 2024–25). "
            "Figures are as-filed (unaudited); they reconcile with Cambridge's audited "
            "reports (see below)."
        )
    )
)
```

Cambridge's reserves have **grown and diversified** over the decade:

- The **total General Fund balance roughly doubled**, from about **$2.5 million
  in 2012–13 to $5.2 million in 2024–25**.
- The district **added reserves it previously did not hold** — a **TRS
  (teachers') retirement** reserve from 2020–21, an **employee-benefit accrued
  liability** reserve, and a substantial **capital reserve** (~$1.1 million) from
  2021–22.
- The **ERS retirement** reserve roughly doubled (to ~$884,000 in 2024–25); the
  **reserve for debt** was drawn down to zero as debt retired.
- The **unassigned, 4%-capped** balance — the flexible part — has been about
  **$1.0–1.2 million** in recent years, sitting at the cap.

## What Cambridge's audited reports show

Cambridge's audited annual financial reports (filed with the Federal Audit
Clearinghouse) confirm and add color to these figures. For **2024–25** they report
**total restricted fund equity of about $2.95 million**, broken down as:

| Reserve (2024–25) | Amount |
|---|---|
| Capital reserves | $1,105,710 |
| Retirement contribution — ERS | $884,019 |
| Retirement contribution — TRS | $663,060 |
| Employee benefit accrued liability | $147,207 |
| Tax certiorari | $138,351 |
| School lunch (encumbrances) | $8,098 |
| **Total restricted** | **$2,946,445** |

This reconciles with the ST-3 figures in the table above (the audited total is
~$8,000 higher, the school-lunch encumbrance). The reports also note the reserves
the board has **appropriated** (budgeted to spend) for 2025–26: **$625,000 from
the capital reserve** and **$200,119 from the employee-benefit reserve**.

The audits flagged two reserve-related items, both since corrected:

- **Tax-certiorari reserve over-funding (finding 2024-002).** The reserve
  exceeded the amount of anticipated tax-certiorari claims. General Municipal Law
  §3651.1-a caps this reserve at anticipated judgments and refunds, and any
  unused balance must be returned to the General Fund. Cambridge corrected the
  over-funding.
- A **capital-assets / net-debt** presentation issue (finding 2024-001, also
  corrected), where invested-in-capital-assets-net-of-related-debt appeared
  negative.

Taken together, Cambridge's reserves are healthy and growing, concentrated in
restricted funds tied to specific future costs (capital projects, pensions),
while its flexible unassigned balance is held at the legal 4% ceiling.
