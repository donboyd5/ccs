Methods & measures

This appendix documents how each measure in the book is defined and its caveats, so the chapters can stay narrative while interested readers can verify the underlying choices. For dataset provenance (publisher, coverage, download lists), see the Sources appendix and the tracked SOURCE.md files under data/raw/.

Conventions used throughout

  • Year labels are school-year-ending. “2025” means the 2024–25 school year. The one exception is the budget-vote chapter, which indexes by the vote’s calendar year (the May 2024 vote is “2024”); the budget adopted there funds the following school year.
  • Enrollment is the BEDS-day count — students enrolled on the first Wednesday of October.
  • Comparison groups. Charts use a 5-district graph group; tables use a 12-district group (every Washington County K-12 district plus Schuylerville and Hoosick Falls). Putnam CSD is excluded because it is K-8 with no high school. See the overview.
  • Charter schools are excluded — they have no district-total row in the source data, so all figures are district-operated.
  • Dollars are nominal — not adjusted for inflation — unless noted. Part of any upward drift in money measures reflects rising prices, not rising real resources.
  • nysed_district_cd (the 8-digit NYSED district code) is the stable join key across datasets; each source’s native id maps to it through a tracked crosswalk. (The budget-vote files are the exception — they carry district names, not codes — so the vote chapter’s Cambridge/neighbor surfacing is by name match.)

Measures and their caveats

Demographics (county population)

  • County population is the July-1 resident total (Census Bureau basis), shown indexed to a base year (2000 = 100, or 2020 = 100) so the lines compare trends across counties of very different sizes, not raw counts.
  • New York history and forecast come from the sibling popfc cohort- component project (baseline scenario); Vermont counties (Bennington, Rutland) come straight from the Census Bureau, which has no popfc forecast. These are the book’s only calendar-year figures — the one place that departs from the school-year-ending convention.
  • Components of change (births, deaths, migration) are shown for Washington County only. The domestic vs international migration split is available only for history (through 2025); the forecast projects just total net migration, so the split columns are blank for forecast years. 2020 is omitted from the components table because the 2020 Census boundary makes that year a partial April–July period in the source. Forecast lines (from 2025) are dashed.

Enrollment and teachers

  • K-12 enrollment — BEDS-day count (above).
  • Teacher counts — a NYSED headcount (the number of people employed as teachers, all grades K-12), not full-time-equivalents. Teacher counts are available from 2017–18, so any series that depends on them (such as students per teacher) begins at school-year-ending 2018, even though enrollment runs back to 2015–16.
  • Students per teacher = K-12 enrollment ÷ teacher headcount. It is an approximation for cross-district and over-time comparison, not NYSED’s official class-size figure and not a count of children in a typical classroom.

Class size

  • NYSED “average class size” is roster/section-based — students in a section ÷ number of sections — reported only for a handful of specific grades and subjects, and only since 2018–19. Where the book summarizes it, it takes the median across the reported classes to resist small-section artifacts (a 2-student physics class, a pandemic-year glitch).
  • It is distinct from the headcount-based students per teacher (above) and the FTE-based NCES ratio (below).

Student test results (grades 3–8 ELA & Math)

  • Proficiency rate = (students at levels 3+4+5) ÷ (students tested). This reproduces NYSED’s own reported proficiency rate exactly in the modern-layout years.
  • Comparability has limits. New York reset its tests and passing standards twice, so rates compare only within three eras — 2015–17 (Common Core), 2018–22 (redesign), 2023–25 (Next Generation) — not across them. A change across an era boundary reflects the new standard as much as any change in learning.
  • 2020 is absent (tests cancelled) and 2021 is anomalous (only about half of students participated; not representative).

Spending per pupil

  • Current operating spending per pupil = (General + Special Aid + School Lunch funds) minus capital outlay, debt service, and interfund transfers, divided by K-12 enrollment. It captures salaries, benefits, classroom and BOCES services, food service, and federally funded programs.
  • What it excludes, and why: capital projects and the debt that funds them arrive in large, irregular lumps (they would make spending per pupil volatile), and interfund transfers would otherwise be counted twice.
  • This is close to the “current spending per pupil” measure the U.S. Census and NCES use. Dollars are nominal.

Property taxes

  • Tax levy — the total property tax the district collects.
  • Full-value tax rate = levy ÷ taxable full value × 1,000 — the standard way to compare tax effort across places with different assessment practices.
  • The rate can move sharply with a reassessment (which expands the tax base and lowers the rate even as the levy rises) — e.g., Washington County’s reassessment around 2022–23.

Property-tax cap

  • A district may raise its levy by the lesser of 2% or CPI without special action; to exceed it the budget must win a 60% supermajority.
  • Each district’s annual levy limit is set by the state formula; certain growth (pension contributions, capital projects, court-ordered payments) can be excluded from the cap.
  • The over/under gap is computed here as proposed levy − levy limit (both excluding permissible exclusions). The source workbook’s stored levy_vs_limit field is not used because NYSED flips its sign at year_end 2016.

Reserves and fund balance

  • Reserve funds are restricted accounts (A814–A884), each created under a specific statute for a specific purpose. Fund balance is classified restricted / committed / assigned (appropriated or not) / unassigned.
  • The 4% cap (tax-cap law) applies to the unassigned fund balance only — not to the restricted reserve funds. So a district can hold large restricted reserves and still be “at the cap.”
  • Per-reserve legal restrictions govern use: the capital reserve requires voter approval to establish and to expend; the tax-certiorari reserve is mandatory, capped at anticipated claims, with unused funds returned to the General Fund; the retirement reserve may be funded up to 2% of covered salaries per year (TRS sub-fund capped at 10%).

Budget votes and contingency budgets

  • A budget passes at ≥60% yes if it is above the cap, else at a simple majority (>50%). A district is on contingency if it failed the first vote and then either failed the re-vote or held none.
  • Cap status comes from NYSED’s “60% required” flag — the vote files carry no levy figures, only that flag.
  • Pre-2012 is omitted (the tax cap took effect in 2012, so the above/below-cap split is undefined before then), and 2020 is atypical (all-absentee, shifted re-vote date).

Cross-checks and validation

  • NYSED headcount vs NCES FTE (students per teacher). The book’s NYSED headcount-based ratio and the independent NCES CCD full-time-equivalent ratio never differ by more than about two-thirds of a student per teacher, and both show the same sharp 2023–24 decline — so that turning point is not an artifact of how NYSED counts teachers.
  • Contingency defeats vs NYSED’s statewide totals. Computed first-vote defeat counts match NYSED’s own published P/D statewide totals exactly for 2021–2025 (within one for the anomalous 2020).
  • OSC ST-3 vs audited reports (reserves). The as-filed OSC account-code reserve balances reconcile with Cambridge’s audited annual financial reports (the ~$8,000 difference is a school-lunch encumbrance).
  • PTRC % Yes reconciliation. Where NYSED’s vote/levy workbooks include a “% Yes” column, the parsed Yes/No counts rebuild it to 0.0000 median error.

Limitations — where comparisons break down

  • No inflation adjustment in money measures — part of the upward drift is prices, not real growth.
  • Small districts are volatile. Cambridge, Salem, and the other small peers have small counts in some cuts of the data (high-school sections, single test grades), so individual values can swing; the book prefers medians and notes this where it matters.
  • Per-pupil spending is mechanically higher for small districts because fixed costs (a principal, a building, a bus route) spread over fewer students — a structural feature of scale, not inefficiency, and central to Cambridge’s story.
  • Name-based matching for budget votes (no codes) means a handful of re-votes each year don’t link to a first-vote record in the older files; re-vote-outcome counts are therefore reliable from ~2020 on and approximate before.
  • Test-score eras and the tax cap each create discrete breaks in their series (above) — trends must be read within an era, not across.
